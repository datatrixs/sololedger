Tax Smart for Creators: Don't Leave Money on the Table

Financial Expert

May 25, 2025 · 9 min read

As a self-employed creator, managing your taxes can feel like navigating a labyrinth. Many creators unknowingly leave thousands of dollars in potential tax savings on the table each year due to confusion around deductions, quarterly payments, and optimal business structures. Verza is designed to demystify creator taxes and help you maximize your earnings.

Understanding Your Tax Obligations as a Creator

When you're a solopreneur or freelancer, you're typically considered an independent contractor. This means you're responsible for both income tax and self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare), which currently stands at 15.3%. Unlike traditional employees, taxes aren't automatically withheld from your payments, making proactive planning essential.

Quarterly Estimated Taxes

The IRS requires you to pay taxes throughout the year if you expect to owe at least $1,000 in tax. Missing these payments can result in penalties.

Self-Employment Tax

This covers your Social Security and Medicare contributions, which are typically split between employer and employee in traditional jobs. As a self-employed individual, you pay both halves.

Deductible Expenses

Many business-related expenses can reduce your taxable income, but only if you track them diligently.

The S-Corporation Advantage: A Game Changer

One of the biggest opportunities for creators to save on taxes is by electing to be taxed as an S-Corporation (S-Corp). While many creators operate as sole proprietors or LLCs, an S-Corp can offer substantial savings, especially if you're earning over $100,000 annually.

How an S-Corp Saves You Money

With an S-Corp, you can pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to self-employment taxes) and take the remaining profits as "distributions." Distributions are not subject to self-employment taxes, only regular income tax. This can lead to thousands of dollars in annual savings.

For example, if you earn $150,000 and pay yourself a $60,000 salary, you'd only pay self-employment tax on the $60,000, not the full $150,000.
