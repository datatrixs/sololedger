Protecting Your IP: A Creator's Essential Guide

Legal Contributor

May 22, 2025 · 10 min read

Your creative work—whether it's a video, a design, a song, or a piece of writing—is your intellectual property (IP). Protecting it is paramount to your career and financial well-being. Without clear terms in your contracts, your IP can be at risk, leading to disputes over ownership, usage, and compensation. Verza helps you navigate this complex legal landscape.

Understanding Key IP Concepts for Creators

Before diving into contracts, it's crucial to grasp the basics of intellectual property:

Copyright

Automatically protects original works of authorship (e.g., videos, music, art, writing). It gives the creator exclusive rights to reproduce, distribute, perform, display, and adapt their work.

Trademark

Protects brand names, logos, slogans, and other identifiers used to distinguish goods or services.

Work for Hire

A critical concept in contracts. If your work is classified as "work for hire," the commissioning party (e.g., brand) is considered the legal author and owner of the copyright from its creation.

Licensing

Granting permission to another party to use your IP under specific terms (e.g., for a certain period, in specific territories, for particular uses) while you retain ownership.

Common IP Traps in Creator Contracts

IP clauses are often where creators get tripped up. Watch out for these common issues:

Broad "Work for Hire" Language

Many contracts attempt to classify all deliverables as "work for hire," meaning you lose all ownership. Verza flags this for negotiation.

Unlimited Usage Rights

A clause granting the brand perpetual, worldwide, irrevocable rights to your content without additional compensation is a major red flag.

Lack of Clear Reversion Rights

What happens to your content rights if the partnership ends? Ensure there's a clear path for rights to revert to you.

Undefined Derivative Works

Ensure the contract specifies if and how the brand can create new works based on yours, and if additional compensation is due.
